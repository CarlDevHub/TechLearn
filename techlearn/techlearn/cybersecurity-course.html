Module 1 — Security Basics · Lesson 1

What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computers, networks, programs, and data from digital attacks, unauthorized access, and damage.

Why Does It Matter?

Every day, individuals and organizations face digital threats:

  • Businesses store sensitive customer data that criminals want to steal.
  • Governments rely on secure systems for national infrastructure.
  • Individuals use banking, healthcare, and communication apps daily.
  • A single breach can cost millions and destroy reputations.

What Cybersecurity Professionals Do

  • Protect — implement security controls and policies
  • Detect — monitor systems for suspicious activity
  • Respond — contain and recover from security incidents
  • Educate — train users to avoid common mistakes
💡 Key insight: The weakest link in any security system is often the human element — not the technology.
Module 1 — Security Basics · Lesson 2

The CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is the foundational model of information security. It defines three objectives every security strategy must address.

1. Confidentiality

Ensures information is accessible only to those authorized to see it.

  • Encryption protects data in transit and at rest
  • Access controls limit who can view sensitive files
  • Example: Your bank only shows your balance to you

2. Integrity

Ensures data is accurate and unaltered unless modified by authorized parties.

  • Hashing verifies files haven't been tampered with
  • Digital signatures prove documents are authentic

3. Availability

Ensures systems and data are accessible when needed.

  • Redundant servers prevent single points of failure
  • DDoS protection keeps websites online under attack
🔐 Remember: Good security balances all three. Focusing on only one creates weaknesses in the others.
Module 1 — Security Basics · Lesson 3

Types of Cyber Threats

Cyber threats come in many forms. Understanding them is the first step to defending against them.

Internal vs External Threats

  • External — hackers, criminal groups, nation-states
  • Internal — malicious or negligent insiders

Common Threat Types

  • Malware — viruses, ransomware, spyware
  • Phishing — deceptive emails to steal credentials
  • Man-in-the-Middle — intercepting communications
  • DDoS — overwhelming a system until it crashes
  • SQL Injection — malicious commands inserted into inputs
⚠️ Threat vs Vulnerability vs Risk: A threat is what could happen. A vulnerability is a weakness. Risk = threat × vulnerability × impact.
Module 2 — Common Attacks · Lesson 1

Phishing

Phishing is a social engineering attack where an attacker impersonates a trusted entity to trick victims into revealing sensitive information.

How It Works

  • Attacker sends a convincing fake email
  • Victim clicks a link to a fake website
  • Victim enters credentials, which the attacker captures

Types of Phishing

  • Spear Phishing — targeted at a specific individual
  • Whaling — targeting senior executives
  • Smishing — phishing via SMS
  • Vishing — phishing via phone calls

How to Spot Phishing

  • Sender's email domain doesn't match the organization
  • Urgent language: "Act now or your account will be locked!"
  • URL doesn't match what's displayed on hover
  • Poor grammar and spelling errors
🛡️ Defence: Always verify unexpected requests through a separate, trusted channel before clicking links.
Module 2 — Common Attacks · Lesson 2

Malware

Malware is any software intentionally designed to cause damage, steal data, or gain unauthorized access to a system.

Types of Malware

  • Virus — attaches to programs and spreads when executed
  • Worm — self-replicates across networks
  • Trojan Horse — disguises itself as legitimate software
  • Ransomware — encrypts files and demands payment
  • Spyware — silently collects user activity
  • Keylogger — records every keystroke

Prevention

  • Keep operating systems and software updated
  • Use reputable antivirus software
  • Avoid downloading from untrusted sources
Module 2 — Common Attacks · Lesson 3

Social Engineering

Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into performing actions or divulging information. It exploits human psychology rather than software vulnerabilities.

Common Techniques

  • Pretexting — fabricated scenario ("I'm from IT, I need your password")
  • Baiting — leaving infected USB drives in public places
  • Quid Pro Quo — offering something in exchange for credentials
  • Tailgating — physically following someone into a secure area
🧠 Key principle: Attackers exploit trust, authority, urgency, and fear. Always verify identity through official channels.
Module 3 — Protection · Lesson 1

Password Security

Passwords are often the first and only line of defence. Weak passwords are one of the leading causes of data breaches.

What Makes a Strong Password

  • At least 12 characters long
  • Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  • Not based on dictionary words or personal info
  • Unique — never reused across accounts
❌ password123 ❌ john1990 ✅ Kx7#mP9!qL2@wR ✅ Coffee!Runs#Before7am (passphrase)

Best Practices

  • Use a password manager (e.g., Bitwarden, 1Password)
  • Enable two-factor authentication everywhere possible
  • Change passwords immediately if a breach is suspected
Module 3 — Protection · Lesson 2

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

2FA adds a second layer of verification beyond your password. Even if an attacker has your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor.

The Three Factors

  • Something you know — password or PIN
  • Something you have — phone, hardware token
  • Something you are — fingerprint, face scan

Types of 2FA

  • SMS codes — easy but vulnerable to SIM-swapping
  • Authenticator apps — Google Authenticator, Authy (more secure)
  • Hardware keys — YubiKey (most secure)
🔑 Use an authenticator app over SMS. Always enable 2FA on email, banking, and social media.
Module 3 — Protection · Lesson 3

Security Best Practices

Keep Software Updated

Updates contain security patches that fix known vulnerabilities. Delaying updates leaves systems exposed.

Use HTTPS

Always look for the padlock and https:// before entering sensitive information.

Principle of Least Privilege

Users should only have access to what they need — nothing more. This limits damage if an account is compromised.

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

  • 3 copies of your data
  • 2 different storage media
  • 1 offsite backup (e.g., cloud)

Create a Password Policy

You've been hired as a junior security consultant for a company with 20 employees. They have no existing password policy. Your task is to create one.

Your Policy Should Cover

  • Minimum password length and complexity
  • Password expiry and rotation rules
  • Rules against password reuse
  • Requirements for multi-factor authentication
  • Acceptable password storage methods
ACME CORP — PASSWORD POLICY v1.0 ================================ 1. Min Length: All passwords must be at least ___ characters. 2. Complexity: Must include uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols. 3. Expiry: Passwords must be changed every ___ days. 4. History: Cannot reuse the last ___ passwords. 5. MFA: Required for all [admin / all / remote] accounts. 6. Storage: Passwords stored using [specify] method only. 7. Sharing: Passwords must NEVER be shared.
💡 There is no single "correct" answer — good policies balance security with usability.
